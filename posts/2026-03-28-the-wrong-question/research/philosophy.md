# Philosophy and Political Theory

This note is organized around the essay's framing question: when vaccination policy is debated as "individual liberty versus collective obligation," what do the major liberal frameworks actually say?

## Rawls and the veil of ignorance in public health

### Did Rawls himself write about public health?

- Not in any detailed way.
- The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy's entry on public-health ethics notes that health does **not** figure prominently in Rawls's original list of primary goods.
- The major Rawlsian extension here is Norman Daniels, who argues that protecting health matters because disease and disability undermine fair equality of opportunity.

### What does a Rawlsian vaccination policy look like?

Behind the veil of ignorance, the parties do not know:

- whether they will be immunocompromised
- whether they will be an infant too young to be vaccinated
- whether they will be medically contraindicated
- whether they will live in a high-contact network
- whether others in their community will cooperate

That strongly pushes a Rawlsian toward a policy that protects the worst-positioned, not merely the median person. In vaccination terms, that usually points toward:

- universal low-cost access
- strong background coverage
- special protection for those who cannot safely vaccinate
- some coercive backup mechanism if voluntary uptake is too low to secure those protections

The maximin logic does not automatically imply "universal criminalized mandate." It implies designing institutions so the worst-off are not left exposed to avoidable epidemic risk.

### How does the maximin principle apply?

If the worst case behind the veil is "I turn out to be vulnerable and dependent on other people's immunity," then a Rawlsian policy tends to favor:

- high baseline vaccination
- narrow medical exemptions
- transparent, public reasons for coercion
- compensation when the policy imposes rare burdens on unlucky individuals

That combination matters. Rawlsian reasoning is not only about pushing coverage up; it is also about making the coercive system fair to the people who bear its residual costs.

### Distinct uncertainty sets

Your prompt is exactly right that "the veil" hides more than one thing. In this domain the unknowns include:

- biological status: am I vulnerable, immunocompromised, pregnant, or medically exempt?
- social position: am I a parent, teacher, clinician, or someone in a dense contact network?
- institutional environment: do I live in a high-coverage or low-coverage community?
- family circumstances: do I have a child who cannot be vaccinated?

I did **not** find a major literature that formally models all of those uncertainty sets together for vaccination. What the literature does show is:

- Daniels-style Rawlsian health justice emphasizes lost opportunity and protection of the vulnerable.
- Public-reason scholarship emphasizes that coercive health policy must be justifiable to free and equal citizens using reasons they can share.
- Vaccine-ethics scholarship often imports Rawlsian ideas indirectly through fairness, reciprocity, and protection of the worst-off rather than through explicit formal veil analysis.

So the essay's move here is partly an inference: it is a Rawlsian extension, not a direct Rawls passage.

### Best Rawlsian framing for the essay

The strongest Rawlsian line is not "everyone must vaccinate because society says so." It is:

> A just society would not ask the vulnerable to bear epidemic risk created by others' optional non-cooperation.

That is Daniels-plus-Rawls rather than Rawls alone, but it is the most defensible way to bring the framework into vaccine policy.

## Mill's harm principle

Mill's classic statement is short and still central:

> "The only purpose for which power can be rightfully exercised ... is to prevent harm to others."

Applied to vaccination, the key question is whether refusal is self-regarding or other-regarding.

### Is non-vaccination self-regarding or other-regarding?

- The "my body, my choice" argument treats vaccination as self-regarding.
- Infectious-disease ethics pushes back because refusal changes the risk environment for others:
  - infants
  - immunocompromised people
  - people for whom the vaccine failed
  - people exposed in schools, clinics, and households

The harder case is not measles in a low-coverage community; it is the marginal refuser inside a highly vaccinated community. There the externality created by one person is small and diffuse. That is where harm-principle debates become subtle.

### The core philosophical split

Mandate critics say:

- one person's refusal rarely causes a directly traceable infection
- the causal link is too diffuse for coercion
- bodily integrity should not be overridden by highly probabilistic social effects

Mandate defenders say:

- infectious-disease harm is cumulative
- public-health law regularly addresses probabilistic and collective harms
- the fact that harm is jointly produced does not make it morally invisible

For essay purposes, the best Millian summary is:

- refusal is **not purely self-regarding**
- but whether that other-regarding risk is large enough to justify coercion depends on severity, transmissibility, context, and the availability of less restrictive tools

## Communitarian versus libertarian arguments

### Strong libertarian case against mandates

The strongest libertarian version is not "vaccines are bad." It is:

- the state should not force medical interventions into competent bodies
- parental authority and bodily integrity deserve a strong presumption
- diffuse statistical externalities are not enough to override that presumption
- once bodily coercion is normalized, the limiting principle becomes unclear

There is also an epistemic version:

- governments can be wrong
- emergency powers are prone to overreach
- centralized policy under uncertainty can impose large coercive errors

This is the version worth engaging because it is principled rather than conspiratorial.

### Communitarian case for mandates or strong conditional requirements

The communitarian argument stresses:

- membership in a shared political community
- reciprocity
- solidarity
- fairness to those who cannot protect themselves

On this view, herd immunity is not an accidental by-product; it is a civic good jointly produced by individual acts. Refusal then looks less like independent lifestyle choice and more like taking the benefits of cooperation without contributing to it.

### Where the mainstream literature lands

The mainstream public-health-ethics position is usually neither pure libertarianism nor simple collectivism. It tends to say coercion is justified when:

- the disease threat is serious
- the vaccine is safe and effective enough
- non-coercive measures are inadequate
- the policy is proportionate
- exemptions are narrow and evidence-based
- those injured by the policy have recourse or compensation

That is why many liberal democracies use school-entry rules, exclusion during outbreaks, or targeted occupational mandates rather than literal forced vaccination.

## Legal and historical precedent

### Jacobson v. Massachusetts (1905)

`Jacobson` remains the canonical U.S. vaccine-mandate case.

The Court upheld a local smallpox vaccination requirement as a valid use of the state's police power. Two lines from the opinion still do most of the work:

> "The liberty secured by the Constitution ... does not import an absolute right."

and

> "real liberty for all could not exist" if each person were free to ignore laws protecting others.

What `Jacobson` stands for in modern usage:

- public-health measures can be constitutional
- liberty is not absolute when conduct creates serious public danger
- courts still care about arbitrariness, necessity, and proportionality

What it does **not** stand for:

- a blank check for any health measure labeled "emergency"
- automatic deference to irrational or discriminatory mandates

### Later liberal-democratic handling

Other liberal democracies usually avoid the language of forced vaccination and instead rely on:

- school or childcare entry conditions
- fines
- exclusion during outbreaks
- occupational requirements

The leading European case is `Vavricka and Others v. the Czech Republic` (ECHR, 2021), where the Grand Chamber held that a Czech childhood-vaccination regime was compatible with the Convention. The court stressed:

- protecting health
- protecting the rights of others
- the state's margin of appreciation
- the best interests of children

That is notable because it frames mandates as compatible with liberal constitutionalism when they are proportionate and aimed at protecting vulnerable people.

## Bottom line for the essay

The philosophical literature does not say "collective good always defeats liberty." It says something sharper:

- Rawlsian approaches focus on the position of the vulnerable and the fairness of institutional design.
- Millian approaches focus on whether refusal creates sufficiently serious risk to others.
- Communitarian approaches stress reciprocity and shared vulnerability.
- Libertarian approaches force the mandate defender to explain why diffuse public risk can justify bodily coercion.

The best synthesis is that **mandates become most defensible when they are embedded in a fair institutional package**:

- free access
- public justification
- narrow exemptions
- proportional enforcement
- compensation for rare harms

That package is where the "individual versus collective" framing starts to weaken.
