# Philosophy and Political Theory

This note is organized around the essay's framing question: when vaccination policy is debated as "individual liberty versus collective obligation," what do the major liberal frameworks actually say?

## Rawls and the Veil of Ignorance in Public Health

### Did Rawls himself write about public health?

- Not in any detailed way. In *A Theory of Justice* (1971), John Rawls focused on the distribution of primary social goods, but health did not figure prominently in his original list.
- The major Rawlsian extension here is **Norman Daniels** (e.g., *Just Health Care*, 1985; *Just Health*, 2008), who argues that protecting health matters because disease and disability undermine "fair equality of opportunity" by limiting an individual's "normal opportunity range."

### What does a Rawlsian vaccination policy look like?

Behind the veil of ignorance, the parties do not know their natural abilities, health status, or social position. Specifically for vaccination, they do not know:

- **Biological status:** whether they will be immunocompromised, an infant too young to be vaccinated, or medically contraindicated.
- **Social position:** whether they will live in a high-contact network, be a teacher, or a clinician.
- **Family circumstances:** whether they will have a child who cannot be vaccinated.
- **Institutional environment:** whether others in their community will cooperate and maintain high coverage.

### How does the maximin principle apply?

The "maximin" principle (maximize the minimum outcome) strongly pushes a Rawlsian toward a policy that protects the worst-positioned, not merely the median person. If the worst case behind the veil is "I turn out to be vulnerable and dependent on other people's immunity," then a Rawlsian policy tends to favor:

- Universal low-cost access and equitable distribution.
- High baseline vaccination.
- Special protection for those who cannot safely vaccinate.
- Some coercive backup mechanism if voluntary uptake is too low to secure those protections.

The strongest Rawlsian line is not "everyone must vaccinate because society says so." It is:
> A just society would not ask the vulnerable to bear epidemic risk created by others' optional non-cooperation.

## Mill's Harm Principle

John Stuart Mill’s "harm principle" is the primary philosophical framework used to justify mandatory vaccination in liberal democracies. His classic statement from *On Liberty* (1859) is short and still central:

> "The only purpose for which power can be rightfully exercised over any member of a civilized community, against his will, is to prevent harm to others. His own good, either physical or moral, is not a sufficient warrant."

### Is non-vaccination self-regarding or other-regarding?

- **Self-regarding actions** (e.g., refusing a life-saving cancer treatment) affect only the individual. The "my body, my choice" argument treats vaccination as self-regarding.
- **Other-regarding actions** affect others. Infectious-disease ethics pushes back because refusal changes the risk environment for others: infants, immunocompromised people, and people for whom the vaccine failed. Because an unvaccinated person can transmit a virus to others or undermine herd immunity, their choice is "other-regarding" and thus subject to state intervention.

### The core philosophical split and modern extensions

Mandate critics say the causal link between one person's refusal and a directly traceable infection is too diffuse for coercion.

Mandate defenders use Mill's logic to argue that vaccination is a moral obligation. Modern extensions include:
- **The "Clean Hands" Principle:** Proposed by philosophers like Jason Brennan (drawing on Mill), this suggests individuals have an enforceable moral duty not to participate in the collective imposition of unjust risk or harm on others. Refusing a vaccine during an outbreak contributes to a collective risk.
- **The Corn-Dealer Example:** Mill famously argued that speech could be restricted if delivered to an "excited mob" outside a corn-dealer's house, posing an immediate threat. Ethicists liken being unvaccinated in a pandemic to this "immediate threat."

For essay purposes, the best Millian summary is that refusal is **not purely self-regarding**, but whether that other-regarding risk is large enough to justify coercion depends on severity, transmissibility, and context.

## Communitarian versus Libertarian Arguments

The debate centers on the tension between collective responsibility and individual autonomy.

### Libertarian Perspective

Libertarianism is rooted in the principle of self-ownership and the Non-Aggression Principle (NAP). 

- **The Strongest Case Against Mandates:** Focuses on **bodily autonomy** (forcing a medical procedure is a fundamental violation of absolute right over one's own body) and **skepticism of state power** (giving the government the power to mandate injections sets a dangerous precedent for future overreach).
- **The Case For Mandates (Academic Libertarians):** Interestingly, some libertarian philosophers (e.g., Jason Brennan, Jessica Flanigan) argue that being unvaccinated in public is akin to "firing a gun into the air." Under the harm principle and NAP, time-limited and narrowly tailored mandates may be justified to prevent the violation of others' rights to life and safety.

### Communitarian Perspective

Communitarianism emphasizes that individuals are part of a larger social fabric. Key thinkers include:

- **Michael Sandel** (*Justice*, 2009; *What Money Can't Buy*, 2012) argues that market logic erodes shared obligations and that health is a common good, not merely a private consumption choice. From this perspective, vaccination is a shared responsibility rooted in our interdependence.
- **Amitai Etzioni**, a leading communitarian theorist, has written directly about vaccination and community responsibility, emphasizing that individuals cannot simply extract the benefits of collective immunity without reciprocal participation.
- **Charles Taylor** and **Michael Walzer** provide broader communitarian frameworks emphasizing the embedded nature of individual choice within social goods and shared meanings.

The communitarian argument proceeds as follows:

- **The Common Good & Social Solidarity:** Vaccination is viewed as a collective effort to achieve "herd immunity," protecting vulnerable members. From a communitarian perspective, vaccination is an act of solidarity—a commitment to "carry the cost" for the well-being of others.
- **Reciprocity:** Because individuals benefit from a disease-free society, they have a reciprocal obligation to contribute to its maintenance. Taking the benefits of cooperation without contributing is free-riding.

### Where the mainstream literature lands

The mainstream public-health-ethics position usually blends these, leaning toward the harm principle. Coercion is justified when the disease threat is serious, the vaccine is safe, non-coercive measures are inadequate, and those injured by the policy have compensation. This avoids pure libertarianism or simple collectivism.

## Legal and Historical Precedent

### Jacobson v. Massachusetts (1905)

*Jacobson v. Massachusetts* (197 U.S. 11) remains the canonical U.S. vaccine-mandate case. Henning Jacobson refused a Cambridge, MA requirement to be vaccinated against smallpox.

The Supreme Court upheld the local smallpox vaccination requirement as a valid use of the state's police power to protect public health and safety. Justice John Marshall Harlan, writing for the majority, delivered this crucial quote:

> "The liberty secured by the Constitution of the United States to every person within its jurisdiction does not import an absolute right in each person to be, at all times and in all circumstances, wholly freed from restraint. There are manifold restraints to which every person is necessarily subject for the common good. On any other basis organized society could not exist with safety to its members. Society based on the rule that each one is a law unto himself would soon be confronted with disorder and anarchy. **Real liberty for all could not exist under the operation of a principle which recognizes the right of each individual person to use his own, whether in respect of his person or his property, regardless of the injury that may be done to others.**"

What `Jacobson` stands for:
- Public-health measures can be constitutional.
- Liberty is not absolute when conduct creates serious public danger.
- Courts still care about arbitrariness, necessity, and proportionality.

### Later liberal-democratic handling

Other liberal democracies usually avoid the language of forced vaccination and instead rely on school or childcare entry conditions, fines, or occupational requirements.

The leading European case is `Vavricka and Others v. the Czech Republic` (ECHR, Grand Chamber, 2021). The court held that a Czech childhood-vaccination regime was compatible with the Convention, stressing the protection of health, the rights of others, the state's margin of appreciation, and the best interests of children.

## Bottom line for the essay

The philosophical literature does not say "collective good always defeats liberty." It says something sharper:

- **Rawlsian** approaches focus on the position of the vulnerable and the fairness of institutional design.
- **Millian** approaches focus on whether refusal creates sufficiently serious risk to others.
- **Communitarian** approaches stress reciprocity and shared vulnerability.
- **Libertarian** approaches force the mandate defender to explain why diffuse public risk can justify bodily coercion (though academic libertarians sometimes accept mandates under the NAP).

The best synthesis is that mandates become most defensible when they are embedded in a fair institutional package.
