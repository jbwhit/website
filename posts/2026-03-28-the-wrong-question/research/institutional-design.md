# Institutional Design and Mandate Mechanisms

This note is organized around the proposed policy ladder:

`recommend -> subsidize -> school-entry requirement -> outbreak-triggered mandate -> universal mandate`

The main conclusion is that real-world systems rarely choose between "pure liberty" and "pure coercion." They usually combine access, conditions, exemptions, compensation, and legitimacy mechanisms.

## The Policy Ladder in Practice

Real-world vaccination systems escalate coercion conditionally. The examples below illustrate how this operates across different jurisdictions:

| Level | Mechanism | Real-world examples | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Recommend | Seasonal influenza vaccination campaigns for healthy adults in many countries. | Typical tools are risk communication, reminders, clinician recommendation, and social norming. Public information campaigns only. |
| 2 | Subsidize | NHS routine childhood vaccines free at point of use in the UK; U.S. Vaccines for Children program. | Removes price barriers even without hard mandates. This is often the default liberal-democratic baseline. |
| 3 | School or workplace entry requirements | U.S. state school-entry requirements (e.g., MMR, DTaP, polio, varicella). | The dominant liberal-democratic mandate form is conditional participation, not forced injection. |
| 4 | Outbreak-triggered mandates / exclusion | New York City measles emergency order in April 2019. | NYC declared a public health emergency targeting four Brooklyn ZIP codes, requiring MMR vaccination or facing a $1,000 fine. |
| 5 | Universal nationwide mandate | France (expanded to 11 mandatory infant vaccines in 2018), Italy (Lorenzin Decree in 2017 expanded to 10 mandatory vaccines), Czech Republic. | Even here, enforcement relies on fines (e.g., €100-€500 in Italy) or school/childcare exclusion, not physical compulsion. Upheld by the European Court of Human Rights in *Vavřička v. Czech Republic* (2021). |

### Which vaccines tend to sit at which levels?

- **Measles/MMR:** Most often levels `3-5` because transmissibility is exceptionally high (R0 ~12-18) and herd immunity thresholds require ~95% coverage.
- **Seasonal influenza:** Usually levels `1-2` for the general public, sometimes level `3` in healthcare or eldercare settings.
- **Childhood DTaP / polio / varicella:** Commonly level `3` in U.S.-style school-entry systems and level `5` in some European systems like France and Italy.

## Vaccine Injury Compensation

No medical intervention is entirely without risk. Compensation programs are institutional mechanisms designed to acknowledge and distribute that residual risk collectively.

### United States: VICP

The U.S. National Vaccine Injury Compensation Program is a federal "no-fault" alternative to the traditional legal system for resolving vaccine injury petitions.

- **How it works:** Claims are filed in the U.S. Court of Federal Claims. It utilizes a "Vaccine Injury Table" listing specific injuries and timeframes. If a condition falls within these parameters, causation is presumed, simplifying the legal process.
- **What it covers:** Medical expenses, lost earnings, and up to $250,000 for pain and suffering or a death benefit. It also covers reasonable legal fees. It covers vaccines routinely recommended for children or pregnant women (e.g., flu, MMR, DTaP, Polio).
- **How it is funded:** The Vaccine Injury Compensation Trust Fund is financed by an excise tax of $0.75 per disease prevented per dose (e.g., the MMR vaccine is taxed at $2.25).

### United Kingdom: Vaccine Damage Payment Scheme

The UK scheme is narrower and less generous than a full tort-compensation model or the U.S. VICP.

- Provides a one-off, tax-free payment of £120,000.
- Available when an individual is severely disabled (at least 60% disabled) as a result of vaccination against specified diseases.
- It is explicitly not a full compensation scheme; claimants retain the right to pursue separate legal action.

### The Role of Compensation in the Mandate Framework

Why do these programs exist?
1. **Stabilize vaccine supply:** By protecting manufacturers from unpredictable liability shocks (a major crisis in the 1980s prompted the creation of the VICP).
2. **Moral reciprocity:** To compensate the unlucky few who are injured while participating in a socially valuable, and often mandated, public health program.
3. **Public willingness:** While empirical evidence on whether these programs directly increase uptake is mixed, normatively, they strengthen the political legitimacy of mandates by sharing the individual burden collectively.

## Exemption Frameworks

Mandates without exemptions are politically and morally fragile. However, exemption design is highly consequential.

### Medical Exemptions
Universally accepted across jurisdictions. They are granted when vaccination would create disproportionate risk (e.g., severe immunodeficiency for live vaccines, documented anaphylaxis). Institutionally, medical exemptions prevent mandates from becoming morally obtuse by recognizing that public health policy must protect vulnerable individuals, not punish them.

### Religious and Philosophical Exemptions
These vary widely by jurisdiction and heavily impact local coverage rates:

- **Religious exemptions:** Allowed in the majority of U.S. states, though some (like New York, following the 2019 measles outbreak) have repealed them due to abuse and public health risks.
- **Philosophical / Personal Belief Exemptions (PBEs):** Allowed in a subset of U.S. states.
- **Impact on coverage rates:** The empirical literature is robust. Systematic reviews (e.g., Wang et al. 2014, building on earlier work by Omer et al. 2006) demonstrate that states with "easier" administrative procedures for nonmedical exemptions have significantly higher exemption rates.
- **Clustering:** Exemptions do not distribute evenly. They cluster geographically, dropping local herd immunity far below the necessary threshold and creating epicenters for outbreaks (especially pertussis and measles), even when statewide averages appear high.

## Legitimacy and Trust

### What makes a mandate legitimate in a liberal democracy?

Mandates risk appearing as state domination unless they meet recurring legitimacy conditions:
- Proportionality to the threat
- Free and convenient access (Subsidization)
- Narrow, evidence-based exemptions
- Compensation for rare harms
- Due process and legally reviewable rules

### Historical Successes vs. Backfires

Mandates are most likely to backfire (generating political resistance or backlash) when they are abrupt, poorly explained, or perceived as punitive rather than protective. Italy's 2017 Lorenzin Decree successfully raised MMR rates from ~87% to over 91%, but it also fueled intense political polarization and "No-Vax" protests that heavily influenced the 2018 national elections.

**The Smallpox Eradication Campaign as a Case Study:**
Smallpox eradication is often framed simply as a triumph of mass compulsory vaccination. However, institutionally, it succeeded through sophisticated design. Early attempts at indiscriminate mass compulsion often faced resistance and logistical failure. The final victory relied heavily on **"ring vaccination"**—intensive surveillance, case finding, and targeted containment around outbreaks. It proved that intelligent, targeted public health infrastructure is often more effective and generates less resistance than brute-force universal coercion alone.

## Key Insight: The Individual vs. Collective False Dichotomy

The strongest institutional-design point for the essay is:

> **Well-designed vaccination systems do not ignore individual concerns; they absorb them.**

They achieve this integration through:
- **Compensation (VICP, VDPS):** Internalizing the financial and moral cost of individual adverse events.
- **Exemptions:** Recognizing genuine individual medical vulnerabilities.
- **Trigger-based escalation:** Using the "policy ladder" to restrict coercion to instances where the collective threat (outbreaks, school environments) strictly necessitates it.

The "individual versus collective" framing is fundamentally a false dichotomy. The most robust institutions do not choose one side; they design mechanisms that secure collective goods (herd immunity) precisely by addressing and internalizing individual burdens.
