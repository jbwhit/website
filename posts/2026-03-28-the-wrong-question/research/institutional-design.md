# Institutional Design and Mandate Mechanisms

This note is organized around your proposed policy ladder:

`recommend -> subsidize -> school-entry requirement -> outbreak-triggered mandate -> universal mandate`

The main conclusion is that real-world systems rarely choose between "pure liberty" and "pure coercion." They usually combine access, conditions, exemptions, compensation, and legitimacy mechanisms.

## The policy ladder in practice

The examples below are illustrative rather than exhaustive.

| Level | Mechanism | Real-world examples | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Recommend | Seasonal influenza vaccination campaigns for healthy adults in many countries; many adult booster recommendations that are encouraged but not legally required | Typical tools are risk communication, reminders, clinician recommendation, and social norming |
| 2 | Subsidize | NHS routine childhood vaccines free at point of use in the UK; U.S. Vaccines for Children program; many national programs that remove price barriers even without hard mandates | This is often the default liberal-democratic baseline |
| 3 | School or workplace entry requirements | U.S. state school-entry requirements for MMR, DTaP, polio, varicella; healthcare-worker vaccination conditions in many systems | The dominant liberal-democratic mandate form is conditional participation, not forced injection |
| 4 | Outbreak-triggered mandates / exclusion | New York City measles emergency orders in 2019; temporary exclusion of unvaccinated students from school during outbreaks in many U.S. states | This level is notable because coercion is conditional and tied to epidemiological triggers |
| 5 | Universal nationwide mandate | Czech childhood-vaccination regime; Italy's 2017 expansion; France's expansion of infant vaccine requirements in 2018 | Even here, enforcement is usually fines or school/childcare exclusion, not physical compulsion |

### Which vaccines tend to sit at which levels?

- Measles/MMR: most often levels `3-5` because transmissibility is so high and herd effects are large.
- Seasonal influenza: usually levels `1-2` for the general public, sometimes level `3` in healthcare settings.
- COVID-19: varied widely by time and jurisdiction, often between levels `1-4`.
- Childhood DTaP / polio / varicella: commonly level `3` in U.S.-style school-entry systems and level `5` in some European systems.

## Vaccine injury compensation

### United States: VICP

The U.S. National Vaccine Injury Compensation Program is a no-fault alternative to ordinary tort litigation.

Core features:

- claims are filed in the U.S. Court of Federal Claims
- covered vaccines are those listed on the Vaccine Injury Table
- petitioners can recover even without proving traditional negligence if the table requirements are met
- the program is funded by a federal excise tax on covered vaccines

Why it exists:

- to stabilize vaccine supply
- to reduce manufacturer liability shocks
- to compensate the unlucky few injured by a socially valuable vaccination program
- to make strong vaccination policy more politically and morally legitimate

Normatively, VICP is one of the clearest examples of the "false dichotomy" dissolving in practice: a collective-immunity system accepts individual residual burdens and builds compensation into the institution.

### United Kingdom: Vaccine Damage Payment Scheme

The UK scheme is narrower and less generous than a full tort-compensation model.

Key features from the current GOV.UK page:

- a `one-off tax-free payment of GBP120,000`
- available when a person is severely disabled because of vaccination against specified diseases
- explicitly **not** a full compensation scheme; claimants can still pursue separate legal action

This is a weaker but still recognizably legitimacy-oriented institutional response: the state acknowledges that rare policy burdens should not fall entirely on unlucky individuals.

### Do compensation programs increase public willingness to accept mandates?

- Normatively: yes, they strengthen legitimacy by acknowledging residual risk and sharing it collectively.
- Empirically: the causal evidence on uptake is thinner than the normative argument. The literature is stronger on legitimacy and fairness than on clean measured effects on vaccination rates.

That is worth saying plainly in the essay.

## Exemption frameworks

### Medical exemptions

Medical exemptions are the least controversial across systems.

They are justified when vaccination would itself create disproportionate risk, for example:

- severe allergic reaction to a vaccine component
- severe immunodeficiency for live vaccines
- pregnancy for some live vaccines

Institutionally, medical exemptions are what keep mandates from becoming morally obtuse. They operationalize the fact that vaccination policy is meant to protect vulnerable people, not punish them.

### Religious and philosophical exemptions

These vary widely by jurisdiction.

In the United States:

- all states allow medical exemptions
- some allow religious exemptions
- some allow philosophical / personal-belief exemptions
- a minority historically allowed only medical exemptions

The major empirical finding is stable:

- easier nonmedical exemptions are associated with lower vaccination coverage and higher outbreak risk

That finding has appeared across multiple reviews of U.S. exemption law.

### Impact on coverage rates

The best general summary is:

- states with easier nonmedical exemptions tend to have higher exemption rates
- higher exemption rates correlate with lower MMR coverage
- local clusters of exemptors are especially important because they create outbreak-prone pockets even when statewide averages look acceptable

This is why many policy reforms do not abolish exemptions wholesale; they make them harder to obtain, more reviewable, or more narrowly tailored.

## Legitimacy and trust

### What makes a mandate legitimate in a liberal democracy?

The recurring legitimacy conditions are:

- proportionality
- transparency
- clear public reasons
- free and convenient access
- narrow, evidence-based exemptions
- due process
- compensation for rare harms
- sunset clauses or epidemiological triggers where appropriate

If those are missing, mandates look like domination. If they are present, mandates look more like a fair system of reciprocal protection.

### Why the policy ladder matters

Your ladder is institutionally realistic because it recognizes escalation.

It says, in effect:

- start with persuasion and access
- remove cost barriers
- use conditional participation rules where warranted
- escalate only when outbreak conditions justify it
- reserve universal mandates for the highest-risk cases

That is much easier to justify in liberal-democratic terms than jumping immediately from recommendation to universal coercion.

### When mandates backfire

Mandates are most likely to backfire when they are:

- abrupt
- poorly explained
- politically polarized
- unevenly enforced
- not paired with easy access and fair exceptions

The backfire risk is real, but it does not imply that all mandates are illegitimate. It implies that mandate design is a first-order policy problem.

## Smallpox eradication as an institutional design case

Smallpox eradication is often remembered as a triumph of vaccination, but institutionally it was a triumph of:

- surveillance
- case finding
- ring vaccination
- local adaptation
- targeted containment

That matters for your essay because it shows that the highest-coercion-looking public-health campaigns often succeeded through a mix of infrastructure and targeted intervention, not by indiscriminate mass compulsion alone.

## Best synthesis for the essay

The strongest institutional-design point is:

> Well-designed vaccination systems do not ignore individual concerns; they absorb them.

They do that through:

- compensation
- exemptions
- transparency
- access
- trigger-based escalation
- legally reviewable rules

That is exactly why the "individual versus collective" framing is often too crude. The most successful institutions do not choose one side. They design mechanisms that protect collective goods while internalizing individual burdens.
