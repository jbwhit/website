## Program Overview

This is a graduate-level reading program spanning military theory, strategy, grand strategy, operations, and the literary dimensions of warfare. Designed for serious self-directed learners, it optimizes for **evaluation over construction**â€”the ability to diagnose, critique, and falsify strategies rather than merely design them.

**Program Statistics:**
- **Year 1**: 12 books (methodology + theory foundations + 2 literature)
- **Year 2**: 19 books (grand strategy + operational art + framework tools + 3 literature)
- **Year 3**: 17 books (contemporary challenges + synthesis + 3 literature)
- **Total: 48 books over 3 years** (~16 per year, ~1.3 per month)

**DIME Framework:** Each text is mapped to the instruments of national power:
- **D** = Diplomatic
- **I** = Informational
- **M** = Military
- **E** = Economic

**Key Design Principles:**
1. **Respects intellectual genealogy** â€” ideas appear before their descendants
2. **Integrates human evidence** â€” literature does epistemic work, not cultural enrichment
3. **Treats strategy as a system** â€” explicit attention to how instruments of power connect
4. **Builds analytical tools early** â€” frameworks appear when most useful, not as capstones
5. **Maintains intellectual tension** â€” countervailing perspectives prevent single-school capture
6. **Privileges failure modes over success narratives** â€” evaluation requires understanding how strategies break

## Study Methodology

This program builds **capability, not just knowledge**. Two companion documents operationalize this:

1. **Reading Journal Template** â€” Complete after each book to extract tools and force decision-relevant thinking
2. **Strategy Evaluation Worksheet** â€” Apply to readings and real-world cases to practice diagnosis and falsification

### The Evaluator's Mindset

The goal is not to finish this program able to design brilliant strategies. The goal is to sit in a room, hear a confident strategic proposal, and say:

> "This will failâ€”and here is exactly where, why, and how."

That requires:
- **Assumption-hunting**: What must be true for this to work?
- **Sensitivity analysis**: Where does the strategy break under stress?
- **Counterfactual reasoning**: What would the adversary do? What if conditions change?
- **Implementation realism**: How will bureaucracies distort this?

### Resist Canon Reverence

Read classics **critically, not reverentially**. Ask:
- What was the author's context and what problem were they solving?
- What has changed since this was written?
- Where is the author's argument weakest?
- What counterfactuals would challenge their conclusions?

### Monthly Review Practice

- **Compare**: How do different authors approach the same problem?
- **Synthesize**: What themes recur across theory, history, and literature?
- **Apply**: Find a current strategic challenge and analyze it using tools from recent readings
- **Question**: Which authors would disagree with each other, and why?

## Year 2: Grand Strategy, Operational Art & Framework Building

### Year 2 Philosophy

Year 2 is the **heaviest intellectual load** but provides the most powerful analytical tools. The sequencing is deliberate:

1. **Clausewitz (complete)** â€” Defines what strategy *is*
2. **Gray** â€” Provides the ends-ways-means-risk evaluation framework
3. **Schelling** â€” Defines how force and coercion *actually work*
4. **Allison** â€” Explains why even good strategies degrade in execution

Only *then* does grand strategy literature become diagnostic rather than inspirational.

**Critical insight**: Gray's *Strategy Bridge* functions as a **red-team instrument**, not a synthesis text. For every case or proposal, ask:
- Are the **ends** politically legitimate and stable?
- Are the **ways** logically connected to those ends?
- Are the **means** adequate, sustainable, and politically tolerable?
- Is **risk** acknowledged, bounded, and owned?

## Year 3: Contemporary Challenges, Critique & Synthesis

### Year 3 Philosophy

Year 3 applies your toolkit to **contemporary strategic challenges** while introducing critical perspectives and non-Western traditions. You begin with powerful analytical frameworks already internalized (Gray, Allison, Schelling), allowing you to critically engage contemporary debates rather than passively absorb them.

**Critical structure**: The irregular warfare section embeds **intellectual tension**:
- **Kilcullen** = the best version of population-centric COIN doctrine
- **Gentile** = direct critique of COIN logic itself
- **Horne** = empirical referee (Algeria case study)

This triangulation trains evaluative thinking: Is the problem tactical, operational, or political? Is adaptation occurring at the right level? Is success being defined militarily when it is actually political?
